# Data Story 03.03.26 
## <a href="https://www.washingtonpost.com/politics/interactive/2026/trump-best-worst-poll-immigration/?itid=hp-top-table-main_p001_f001">We asked 2,300 Americans about the best and worst things Trump has done.</a>
### Publication: Washington Post
### Publication Date: 02.24.26
This article did a good job of breaking down the overarching question of how Americans perceive Trump's actions. Through surveys, they asked respondents to answer a simple yes or no question on whether they approved or disapproved. Respondents then had to rank what they thought were the best or worst things Trump has done from a set list, of which they could choose up to three things. The reporters did a good job of displaying these responses in an engaging way through their visualizations and percentages, but I wonder how the three-answer maximum skews this data. Were respondents allowed to rank the three or just choose? However, I did like how the Post highlighted several free-response answers to hint at anecdotal aspects of the survey and emphasize the two polarizing sides of his supporters and opposers. Beyond just gender, state, age, and political party, I would have also included race and/or ethnicity to paint an even more vivid picture of who approves of Trump's actions and who does not. The Post also does a good job of always explaining how they gathered their data with an explicit section at the bottom.
