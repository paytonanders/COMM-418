# Data Story 2026.03.09
## <a href="https://www.wsj.com/world/middle-east/strait-of-hormuz-the-oil-bottleneck-threatening-the-global-economy-7d221c51?mod=WSJ_home_supertoppermiddle_pos_4">Strait of Hormuz: The Oil Bottleneck Threatening the Global Economy</a>
### Publication: The Wall Street Journal 
### Date: 2026.03.04 
The recent war with Iran has severely disrupted shipping through the Strait of Hormuz, trapping thousands of vessels and threatening roughly one-fifth of global oil and gas supplies. This article shows how production cuts, tanker attacks, and soaring transport costs have pushed oil prices higher. The WSJ reporters initially used a line graph to show that oil has increased in price by 13 percent, and the cost of chartering a large crude carrier from the Gulf Coast to Asia has also spiked. The reporters also used a bar graph to show that Saudi Arabia was by far the leading crude oil producer, compared to other Gulf Nations, in 2024. These pieces of data better inform the international story in the eyes of a US audience, explaining how the disruption of oil production and transportation will directly impact the global industry. Although not a very data-heavy story, I liked how the reporters used data and graphs to aid in their explanations. Rather than contextualizing the data like most data stories do, the data actually contextualized the sourcing by explaining why these disruptions matter. Using a bar graph was especially useful in showing where other Gulf Nations are in the energy production industry.
